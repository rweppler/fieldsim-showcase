01 — the route card

Camps on the prize mountain

A DraftKings-style GPP doesn't pay a slope, it pays a staircase carved into a cliff face. Twenty percent of entries cash, and the bottom half of those barely double the entry fee — that's the long, flat approach march. The real mountain begins around the final table.

FieldSim prices every simulated lineup against this exact staircase. A lineup's worth isn't its points — it's where those points land on this terrain, fifty thousand Sundays in a row, prizes split with every duplicate standing on the same ledge.

02 — the doctrine

Top-heavy terrain makes variance an asset

On flat terrain — a 50/50, a double-up — the smart play is the safe play: take the highest floor and walk. On a cliff, floors are worthless. The distance between rank 100 and rank 1 is a hundredfold, and no floor climbs it. Only ceilings do.

This is why every FieldSim ranking is denominated in ROI against this curve, never in mean points. A lineup that wins the week 0.8% of the time can be worth more than one that cashes twice as often — because the summit pays for a season of approach marches.

And it's why duplication is priced in. Stand on the summit with three copies of your lineup and the flag is worth a third. The engine gives every field lineup an expected copy count and splits each ledge among everyone standing on it — chalk pays a mountain toll precisely where the mountain is steepest.

The result: an entry-weighted mean ROI of exactly −rake, conserved to a rounding error. The mountain gives nothing away; it only redistributes — from the crowded ledges to the empty ones.
