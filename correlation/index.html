The matrix is the machine

Under every FieldSim contest sits a player-by-player correlation matrix. Same-team wires are warm: a quarterback and his №1 receiver share completions, so they share ceilings (one deep ball = two stat lines!). The wire to the opposing defense runs negative — every sack that feeds the DST starves the QB.

And the two quarterbacks? Positive. Points beget points: a shootout lifts both passing games, which is why a bring-back — one opposing receiver bolted onto your stack — is wired into the engine, not bolted on as a rule of thumb.

Hand-set matrices are almost never mathematically valid. FieldSim repairs every matrix to its nearest positive-semi-definite neighbour before sampling — otherwise the copula would be asked to draw from a correlation structure that cannot exist in any universe, simulated or otherwise.

02

Wiring rules of the trade

Every wire on this sheet ships in the production engine's config — these are the defaults the simulator draws through, not illustrations.

W-01

The primary tether

QB ↔ WR1, same team: ρ +0.62. The single strongest wire on the board. Stacking is just obeying it.

W-02

The bring-back

Your stack ↔ opposing WR1: ρ +0.35 via the shootout. If the game goes nuclear, own both sides of the mushroom cloud.

W-03

The negative wire

QB ↔ opposing DST: ρ −0.45. A defense scores by eating your quarterback. Never roster the meal and the mouth.

W-04

The quiet couple

RB ↔ own DST: ρ +0.25. Leads make runs and rested defenses — the grind-it-out correlation everyone forgets.

W-05

The cannibals

QB ↔ own RB: ρ −0.08. Slightly negative — every kneel-down drive is a passing ceiling that never happened.

W-06

The shootout switch

QB ↔ QB: ρ +0.28. Scoreboards are contagious. Game environment is a player in every lineup, priced or not.
