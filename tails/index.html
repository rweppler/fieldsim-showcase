NOT NORMAL

Every projection system hands you a mean. The bell curve then whispers a lie: that a player misses high as often as he misses low, by the same amount. Football does not work that way, and neither does this engine — every FieldSim marginal is a right-skewed shifted lognormal, fit to the boom and the bust, tails capped at what an NFL Sunday can physically pay.

Bell curves refused at the door

Argument 01

The floor is concrete. The ceiling is paper.

A running back cannot score minus eleven. The floor of a football Sunday is poured concrete: zero targets, a lost fumble, a benching — the damage bottoms out fast. The ceiling is different material. One broken tackle is fifteen points. A blown coverage is six more. The distance from median to disaster is short; the distance from median to miracle is a country mile.

Pile up ten thousand real box scores and the shape is unmistakable: mass crowded against the floor, a long paper tail streaming right. A normal distribution splits its errors evenly. Football doesn't split anything evenly — it hoards downside and rations upside.

Argument 03

The recipe, since you'll ask.

This is the actual fitting procedure inside the engine — not a sketch of one. Five observable numbers go in; one honest distribution comes out. The thresholds that define "boom" and "bust" are calibrated from the slate itself — the folk rule of "5× value" is mathematically impossible for this data, so the engine refuses to hardcode it.

Players are never modeled as normal

Shifted lognormal, house cut

1

Take the slate's projection, std dev, ceiling, and measured Boom% / Bust%.

2

Auto-calibrate the boom/bust thresholds from the data — never from folklore.

3

Fit X = shift + LogNormal(μ, σ) so mean, sd AND both tail probabilities match at once.

4

Cap the far tail at realistic NFL maxima — no 90-point receivers, even at p99.99.

5

Hand the marginal to the copula. Rank-remap every correlated sim back onto it, so dependence never distorts the fit.
